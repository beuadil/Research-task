## Recommendations

I spent time going through posts, videos, and case studies from 10 practitioners who actually run SEO campaigns with AI — not people who just write about it. These are the things that came up repeatedly and actually make sense when you look at how search works now across both Google and AI tools like Perplexity and ChatGPT.

### Never prompt AI for a full article in one shot — do outline first, then sections

Ask AI for the outline first. Get that right. Then go section by section using the outline as the reference. When you prompt for the full article at once, the output is generic and structurally all over the place. The two-step approach consistently produces something you can actually work with. (source: Matt Diggity, "I Let AI Run My SEO", [youtube.com/watch?v=xG9Vu9yrQzw](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xG9Vu9yrQzw), 29.10.2025)

### Every piece needs at least one thing AI physically cannot write

A real data point from your own work. An actual quote from someone you interviewed. A screenshot from a campaign you ran. Something that proves a human with real experience was involved. If the whole article could have been written by AI without any input from you, it probably shouldn't be published. "If AI can easily create it, don't publish it. Add what AI can't fake." (source: Nathan Gotch, "6 AI SEO Skills That Matter Most 2026", [youtube.com/watch?v=mbCfRlY7elM](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mbCfRlY7elM), 19.01.2026)

### Map the full topic before writing anything

Don't pick one keyword and start writing. Figure out the whole topic — main pillar, subtopics, FAQ-level questions — and then work through it systematically. AI search doesn't rank isolated pages the way old Google did. It needs to see that you cover a topic properly. "You're optimizing for comprehensive, well-structured content that AI can flexibly extract from any accessible page, rather than rigid query-to-page matching." (source: Aleyda Solis, [post from 05.02.2026](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/aleyda_ai-search-where-are-we-so-far-and-how-activity-7425156088175689728-lIa-))

### Track AI visibility separately — don't assume Google rankings = AI citations

Every month, run your 10 most important queries in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini. Record whether you show up, where, and who your competitors are in those answers. This is not the same as your Google rankings. "Don't assume that SEO wins = AI wins. Relying solely on traditional SEO data could mean you're invisible where it counts." (source: Kevin Indig, post from 01.02.2026 — link unavailable)

### After every AI draft, check the structure manually

AI writes readable content. It does not reliably produce the right structure for ranking. In a study that tested 13 LLMs, only 1 out of 13 hit the 80+ score needed to be competitive — and the failures were all structural: wrong heading count, wrong content depth, missing entity terms. Adding "SEO-optimized" to your prompt doesn't fix it. You have to check it yourself. "AI produces readable content but search engines rank measurable signals." (source: Kyle Roof, "We Tested 13 LLMs for SEO Content in 2026", [pageoptimizer.pro/blog/we-analyzed-9-major-llms-heres-which-llm-is-best-for-seo](https://www.pageoptimizer.pro/blog/we-analyzed-9-major-llms-heres-which-llm-is-best-for-seo), 12.03.2026)

### Getting mentioned matters more than getting linked

Find which sites show up most often in AI citations for your topic. Then get your brand mentioned there — as a reference, a quote, anything. Links still matter for Google, but unlinked brand mentions now influence what AI tools recommend. "It doesn't seem to matter if it's follow, no follow, or even an unlinked mention. The influence still shows up." (source: Nathan Gotch, "6 AI SEO Skills That Matter Most 2026", [youtube.com/watch?v=mbCfRlY7elM](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mbCfRlY7elM), 19.01.2026)

### Put a short direct answer right under each heading, and a TL;DR at the top

40–60 words max. Answer the question immediately, before any context or explanation. AI systems pull these for citations. Google pulls them for featured snippets. Both reward the same format. "AI also really likes to cite key takeaway sections." (source: Matt Diggity, "I did AI-SEO for ChatGPT and Google AI. Here's what happened…", [youtube.com/watch?v=4GBlHObjOrY](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4GBlHObjOrY), 18.06.2025)

### Build a knowledge base for every brand before touching AI prompts

Before you write a single piece of AI-assisted content for a brand, compile the basics: messaging, offers, FAQs, case studies, objections, customer language. Feed this into every prompt. Otherwise AI defaults to generic output that sounds like it could be about any company. "A knowledge base improves the quality of your AI assisted content and makes you faster at high leverage work." (source: Nathan Gotch, "6 AI SEO Skills That Matter Most 2026", [youtube.com/watch?v=mbCfRlY7elM](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mbCfRlY7elM), 19.01.2026)

### Make sure your brand information is consistent everywhere AI looks

Google Business Profile, LinkedIn, Trustpilot, G2, niche directories — whatever is relevant for your industry. The name, description, and what you do should be identical across all of them. AI systems cross-reference these sources. Inconsistency reads as a trust problem. A home services client went from 0 to 90 AI Overview appearances after doing exactly this, alongside a 2,300% increase in AI referral traffic. (source: Matt Diggity, "AI SEO Case Study: +3.7x Monthly AI Traffic", [diggitymarketing.com/ai-seo-traffic-case-study/](https://diggitymarketing.com/ai-seo-traffic-case-study/), 29.09.2025)

### Set a 6-month refresh trigger for every article in the top 10

AI search surfaces prefer recent content. A strong 2023 article can quietly lose citation ground to a weaker 2025 article covering the same topic. Use AI to find what's outdated and what competitors are now covering that you aren't. "Updating blog content is one of the highest-ROI activities in content marketing." (source: Ryan Law, [post from 16.03.2026](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/thinkingslow_ai-content-wasnt-good-enough-now-it-is-activity-7439350229327302656-3JNC))

## Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Is AI search optimization a new thing, or just SEO with a new name?

**Ryan Law says** it's just SEO. The way you improve LLM visibility is the same as traditional SEO — create relevant content, rank in the indexes LLMs use for RAG, which are Google and Bing. He asked publicly if anyone can show a brand with strong AI visibility but weak Google rankings, or vice versa. Nobody could.

> "LLMO, GEO, LEO — whatever acronym you like — it's just SEO. Can anyone show me a company who has great visibility in LLMs but not in traditional search? Or vice versa?"

(source: Ryan Law, [post from 13.03.2025](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/thinkingslow_im-just-gonna-say-it-llmo-geo-leo-whatever-activity-7305908021312782338-uHit))

**Kevin Indig says** the gap is real and you'll miss it if you're not measuring separately. He ran an analysis on Shopify and found that AI tools were citing Shopify mostly for "motivation and lifestyle" content (~43%) rather than as a software tool (~14%), despite Shopify ranking well on Google for commercial terms. Good Google rankings and accurate AI characterization are not the same thing.

> "In the age of LLMs, the more critical question is who the AI thinks we are. I call this your AI Brand Archetype."

(source: Kevin Indig, [post from 01.02.2026](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kevinindig_in-seo-we-obsess-over-where-we-rank-but-activity-7423732576139476992-sx3r))

**My take: Kevin is right in practice.** Ryan's logic works at the structural level — the same underlying signals drive both. But the Shopify data shows they can diverge significantly for real brands. If you assume your SEO health automatically covers your AI visibility, you'll never catch a problem like that. Measure them separately.

---

### 2. Should AI write the full draft, or just help with parts?

**Matt Diggity says** AI should write full drafts at scale for non-critical content — informational pages, how-tos, category pages. His Make.com + ChatGPT pipeline produced 300 articles in batch that ranked. The point is to reserve human writing for pages that actually drive revenue.

> "You don't need to be Shakespeare to write recipe content. You can turn out hundreds of articles for your non-critical content at scale while leaving your high priority sales pages to your human writers."

(source: Matt Diggity, "I Let AI Run My SEO", [youtube.com/watch?v=xG9Vu9yrQzw](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xG9Vu9yrQzw), 29.10.2025)

**Ryan Law says** full automation is the wrong frame even when AI quality is good enough. The Ahrefs content machine uses AI throughout but keeps humans in the loop at every meaningful decision point. The framing from their announcement was clear: "That's not a one-click easy-peasy process. It requires quite a bit of work and skill to pull off." (source: Tim Soulo referencing Ryan Law's AI content machine, [post from 05.08.2025](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/timsoulo_heres-the-10-step-ai-content-machine-devised-activity-7358487440740790273-OoHe))

**My take: depends on what's at stake.** Matt's model is fine for an affiliate site where no single article carries brand weight. For any company where published content represents the business — a SaaS, an agency, a personal brand — publishing things you haven't properly reviewed is a liability. One bad piece on a commodity site costs nothing. One bad piece that gets shared when you're trying to build credibility costs a lot. Simple filter: does this site have anything to lose from a mediocre piece going out? If yes, don't automate without review.

### 3. Topical authority or backlinks — what actually drives rankings?

**Koray Tugberk Gubur says** topical authority is the organizing principle of modern search and AI search. Google and LLMs both evaluate how thoroughly a domain covers a topic space. He's been saying this since before LLMs became mainstream, and the way AI retrieval works now validates it.

> "If you understand semantics, NLP, and information retrieval, you're already in the right lane. Just stay rooted in research and reason — you'll be years ahead of the rest."

(source: Koray Tugberk Gubur, [post from 15.04.2025](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/koray-tugberk-gubur_seo-activity-7317944506811670528-nxIu))

**Matt Diggity says** backlinks still decide competitive rankings. His AI Overviews case study included high-quality link acquisition as a core part of the campaign — not a nice-to-have.

(source: Matt Diggity, "I did AI-SEO for ChatGPT and Google AI. Here's what happened…", [youtube.com/watch?v=4GBlHObjOrY](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4GBlHObjOrY), 18.06.2025)

**My take: both are right, but for different environments.** For low-competition informational topics, topical coverage is usually enough. For competitive commercial niches, links are still the tiebreaker. Long-term, I lean toward Koray's framework being more important — AI search weighs topical comprehensiveness more like he describes than like traditional PageRank. Ignoring links today is still a mistake. Treating them as the main lever in five years probably will be too.

## What I Rejected and Why

### 1. Perplexity Parasite SEO as a main strategy

Julian Goldie's approach is to publish content on high-authority third-party platforms — Medium, LinkedIn, Reddit — specifically to get cited by Perplexity. He shows real citation results with it. (source: Julian Goldie, "How to Rank #1 with Perplexity Parasite AI SEO in 2026!", [youtube.com/watch?v=WhLyFjzbzvQ](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WhLyFjzbzvQ), 03.01.2026)

I'm not including it because you're building on platforms you don't own. Perplexity changes its citation logic without warning. Reddit has restricted SEO content multiple times. Medium buries it algorithmically. Whatever visibility you build can disappear with one policy update. The other issue is that Julian's videos are sales funnels — both end with calls to buy his course or hire his agency. That creates an incentive to show tactics that look impressive fast, not tactics that hold up over two years. Parasite SEO fits the demo format well and breaks in production. I don't want to build on that.

### 2. High-volume automated publishing with no human review

Matt Diggity's batch pipeline actually worked for a home interiors client — the traffic numbers were real. He's also upfront about what it produces: "Is it the best content on the internet? No, not even close." (source: Matt Diggity, "I Let AI Run My SEO", [youtube.com/watch?v=xG9Vu9yrQzw](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xG9Vu9yrQzw), 29.10.2025)

I'm not including it as a general recommendation because Google's March 2024 core update directly targeted scaled content abuse. Sites running fully automated pipelines without review took real ranking hits. Beyond the algorithm risk — publishing things you haven't read means errors go out. Wrong facts, broken logic, off-brand claims. The risk isn't worth it. Ryan Law put it well: AI made mediocre content cheap, which means the advantage now goes to whoever is consistently above that bar, not whoever produces the most of it. (source: Ryan Law, "14 Ways to Use AI for Better, Faster SEO", [ahrefs.com/blog/ai-seo/](https://ahrefs.com/blog/ai-seo/), 16.05.2024)

## My Original Ideas

### The Citable Claim Framework

Most content workflows start with a keyword, then a brief, then a draft. What I think is missing is a step before all of that: deciding what original, citable claim the piece will be built around.

A citable claim is something specific — a number, a finding, a result from your own data — that a journalist, another writer, or an AI tool would want to reference. Not a general observation. Something concrete enough to quote.

**Example:** Instead of briefing "email marketing best practices," you first pull a data point from your own campaigns: *"We looked at 800 cold email sequences and found 4-day intervals produced 18% higher reply rates than 3-day ones."* Then you write the article around that finding.

**Why none of the sources do this:** Kevin Indig gets closest — his AI Brand Archetype work shows that LLMs cite brands that produce original, specific claims. Bernard Huang's point that "mentions are the new rankings" points in the same direction. (source: Bernard Huang, [post from 18.11.2025](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/bernardjhuang_the-biggest-shift-in-seo-isnt-technical-activity-7396614567365263360-oUw4)) But nobody treats it as a production-first decision. They add original research on top of articles that already exist, rather than using it as the reason an article gets written in the first place.

**Why I think it works:** AI tools and journalists cite specific quantified claims, not summaries of what other people said. If you start from "what original finding can we produce?" instead of "what keyword should we target?", you get both the research signal that builds authority and the context around it that ranks. And citations compound — each one makes the next one more likely. The practical version of this: keep a Claims Bank, a running list of original data points your team generates. Check it before briefing any article. If there's nothing relevant in it for a topic, produce something before writing.

## Weaknesses of This Playbook

**The sources are all practitioners, not researchers.** Everyone cited here is sharing results from their own clients or experiments. None of it is controlled or peer-reviewed. The people who ran failed campaigns aren't making YouTube videos about it — so what I'm working with is survivorship bias by design. Some of these experts also sell tools tied directly to the tactics they recommend: Kyle Roof sells PageOptimizer Pro, Bernard Huang sells Clearscope, Nathan Gotch sells Rankability. I tried to weight people who don't have a product attached to every recommendation, but the conflict of interest is there throughout.

**The AI search tactics will go stale fast.** Perplexity, ChatGPT Search, and Google AI Overviews change their citation logic and interface constantly. What I've written about AI visibility tracking and brand mentions reflects how these platforms work in April 2026. By Q4 it may look different. The strategic direction — build real authority, produce original claims, track AI separately — will hold. The specific tactics are more fragile.

**This only works if you have someone who can actually edit.** The whole thing depends on a human editorial layer that most small teams don't have capacity for. It assumes someone who can write well, spot weak AI output, and add genuine expertise. If that person doesn't exist on the team, the AI drafting steps in this playbook produce exactly the kind of average content it argues against publishing.

**The Citable Claim Framework hasn't been tested.** I haven't run a controlled experiment comparing "claim-first" content production to "keyword-first" on real citation or traffic metrics. The reasoning makes sense to me, but I don't have data for it yet. It's a hypothesis.

**No local SEO, e-commerce, or video.** This was built for informational content sites and SaaS blogs. Product pages, local businesses, and YouTube require different approaches that aren't covered here.

## Who I Would NOT Recommend Following and Why

### Julian Goldie

He finds new tactics early and understands the technical side. His videos on Perplexity citation optimization were out before most SEOs were paying attention to it. I'm not saying he's wrong about everything.

The problem is that both of his videos in this research are structured as sales funnels. Each one ends with multiple pitches to join his paid SEO Elite Circle or hire his agency. That's not a minor detail — it means every recommendation he makes is filtered through "does this make a good demo?" and "does this make someone want to buy my course?" Those incentives produce different advice than someone who is just trying to give you an accurate picture.

The way he talks confirms it. "Rank #1 in days, not months." "This is huge." "Way easier than you think." (source: Julian Goldie, "How to Rank #1 with Perplexity Parasite AI SEO in 2026!", [youtube.com/watch?v=WhLyFjzbzvQ](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=WhLyFjzbzvQ), 03.01.2026)

Compare that to Ryan Law, who will tell you when AI content has limits. Or Aleyda Solis, who publishes what didn't work alongside what did. Or Nathan Gotch, who explicitly flags when a tactic doesn't apply. Julian doesn't do that. There are no caveats, no failure cases, no "this won't work if." That's not how someone communicates when accuracy is the goal.

The specific thing that crossed a line for me: calling Perplexity Parasite SEO "the biggest shift in search since Google launched" is not a difference of opinion. It's a description no honest practitioner would use for a borrowed-authority tactic built on platforms you don't control. That's just hyperbole for conversion purposes.

Use him to spot emerging tactics early. But run a small experiment before scaling anything he recommends, and don't take the success claims at face value until you've replicated them yourself.
